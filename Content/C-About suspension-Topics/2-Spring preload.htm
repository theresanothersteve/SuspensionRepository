Spring preload

Most people are familiar with springs. They range in size from minuscule, such as in a watch, to huge.

Spring types

Springs come in several different forms. They all store energy and release it once the load is reduced. There is some energy lost through internal friction that is converted to heat.

A spring can be a flat piece of steel that bends and returns to its original shape. There must be an eye that attaches to a pivot and shackle on at least one end to allow the spring to bend. Several can be added together. This sort of spring is a leaf spring.

Springs can also be a bar, bent or otherwise offset on each end. Pushing on the bent arm causes the straight piece in the middle to twist. This type of spring is a torsion bar.

There are springs that are wound into a spiral, and pull back when released, such as a tape measure. They can also push back, such as a watch spring.

The most common spring on motorcycle suspension is a coil spring. Metal is wound in a coil. Compression coil springs have gaps between the coils and resist being pushed together, while extension springs have no gaps and resist being pulled apart. Coils can be seen externally, wrapped over or around something, or they can be housed inside tubing (think motorcycle forks) and out of sight.

Spring rates

The characteristics of a spring are expressed by how far they move under a specific load. Or, more precisely, the amount of force necessary to compress a spring a set distance.

Spring rates are usually expressed in metric (kilogram per millimetre) or imperial (pounds per inch). If the spring is rated at 10 kilograms per millimetre it means if you apply 10 kilograms of weight to the top of the spring, it will compress 1 millimetre. 20 kilograms, 2 millimetres. 50 kilograms, 5 millimetres. And so on until the spring cannot compress any more.

This example is using a linear spring rate. There are also progressive springs, identifiable by an uneven winding. The top of the spring is narrowly wound and the coils get further apart towards the base. The more the spring compresses, the higher the spring rate becomes.

This might sound like a good idea but the results are usually too unpredictable. It is uncommon for high end suspension to use progressive springs.

Identifying spring rate

You cannot identify spring rate just by measuring the spring diameter, material thickness, or coil spacing. The only way is to look at the manufacturer's markings or place weight on the spring to see how far it compresses.

Springs can be made from a variety of materials including steel, titanium, carbon fibre etc. Depending on the quality of the materials used a heavy coil spring with closely wound coils might have the same spring rate as a spring from a different manufacturer that is from smaller diameter stock with widely spaced coils. While the second spring sounds better because it is lighter it will probably cost far more to make.

Spring preload

When a spring is installed in the suspension (whether it is internal in the forks or an external coil over on the rear) the spring is usually slightly compressed. The distance between the spring's free length to its installed length when the suspension is fully extended is spring preload.

The spring will exert a force on the end of the fork tube or adjusting ring on a rear shock. This force is preload force.

It takes the same amount of force as the preload force before the suspension will start to move. If the preload force is 5 kilograms, then 5 kilograms of force is needed. If the spring rate is 10 kilograms per millimetre and it has 5 kilograms of preload force it means if you apply 10 kilograms of weight to the top of the spring, it will compress 0.5 millimetres.

The preload needs to be adjusted so the mass of the motorcycle supported by the spring, plus the weight of the rider, compresses the spring the right amount, typically about 1/3 of the suspension's travel.
